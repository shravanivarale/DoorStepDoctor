# Fever Triage Protocol for Rural Healthcare

## Document Information
- **Source**: WHO Rural Triage Guidelines
- **Version**: 2.1
- **Last Updated**: January 2024
- **Category**: General Triage

## Overview
This protocol guides ASHA workers in assessing patients presenting with fever in rural settings.

## Urgency Classification

### Emergency (Immediate Referral Required)
- Temperature > 104°F (40°C)
- Fever with altered consciousness or confusion
- Fever with severe headache and neck stiffness (possible meningitis)
- Fever with difficulty breathing or chest pain
- Fever with severe dehydration (unable to drink, no urine output)
- Fever in infant < 3 months old
- Fever with seizures
- Fever with severe abdominal pain

**Action**: Immediate referral to PHC or district hospital. Call emergency services (108).

### High Priority (Same Day PHC Visit)
- Temperature 102-104°F (39-40°C) for > 3 days
- Fever with persistent vomiting
- Fever with severe body aches
- Fever with rash
- Fever in pregnant women
- Fever with jaundice (yellow eyes/skin)
- Fever with blood in urine or stool

**Action**: Refer to PHC within 4-6 hours. Monitor closely.

### Medium Priority (PHC Visit Within 24-48 Hours)
- Temperature 100-102°F (37.8-39°C) for 2-3 days
- Fever with mild cough and cold symptoms
- Fever with mild body aches
- Fever with loss of appetite
- Fever responding to home remedies but not resolving

**Action**: Advise PHC visit within 24-48 hours. Provide home care guidance.

### Low Priority (Home Care with Monitoring)
- Temperature < 100°F (37.8°C)
- Fever for < 2 days with no other symptoms
- Fever responding well to home treatment
- Patient able to eat and drink normally

**Action**: Home care with monitoring. Advise when to seek medical help.

## Red Flags (Always Escalate)
1. Fever with altered mental status
2. Fever with severe headache and vomiting
3. Fever with difficulty breathing
4. Fever in very young infants (< 3 months)
5. Fever with seizures or convulsions
6. Fever with severe dehydration
7. Fever with bleeding from any site

## Seasonal Considerations

### Monsoon Season (June-September)
- High risk: Dengue, Malaria, Leptospirosis
- Look for: Joint pain, rash, bleeding tendencies
- Escalate if: Platelet count concerns, severe body aches

### Summer Season (March-May)
- High risk: Heat stroke, Typhoid
- Look for: Extreme weakness, confusion, dehydration
- Escalate if: Temperature > 104°F with confusion

### Winter Season (November-February)
- High risk: Influenza, Pneumonia
- Look for: Cough, difficulty breathing, chest pain
- Escalate if: Breathing difficulty, chest pain

## Home Care Advice (Low Priority Cases)
1. Rest and adequate sleep
2. Drink plenty of fluids (water, ORS, coconut water)
3. Light, easily digestible food
4. Sponge with lukewarm water if temperature > 100°F
5. Monitor temperature every 4-6 hours
6. Avoid cold water baths

## When to Return for Reassessment
- Fever persists > 3 days
- Temperature increases > 102°F
- New symptoms develop
- Patient unable to eat or drink
- Severe weakness or drowsiness
- Any red flag symptoms appear

## Documentation Requirements
- Record temperature
- Duration of fever
- Associated symptoms
- Home treatments tried
- Patient's ability to eat/drink
- Any red flag symptoms

## Important Notes
- **DO NOT** recommend specific medications or dosages
- **DO NOT** diagnose specific diseases
- **ALWAYS** refer to PHC for proper diagnosis and treatment
- **DOCUMENT** all assessments and referrals
- **FOLLOW UP** on referred cases
