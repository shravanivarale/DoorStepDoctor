# Fever Management Protocol

## Temperature Classification

**Normal Body Temperature:**
- Oral: 97-99°F (36-37.2°C)
- Axillary (armpit): 96.6-98.6°F (35.9-37°C)
- Rectal: 98.6-100.4°F (37-38°C)

**Fever Categories:**
- **Low-grade fever:** 99-100.4°F (37.2-38°C)
- **Moderate fever:** 100.4-103°F (38-39.5°C)
- **High fever:** 103-106°F (39.5-41°C)
- **Hyperpyrexia:** >106°F (>41°C) - MEDICAL EMERGENCY

## Comprehensive Assessment Questions

**Ask these questions systematically:**

1. **Duration:** How long has fever been present? (hours/days)
2. **Pattern:** Continuous or comes and goes? Worse at certain times?
3. **Highest temperature:** What was the highest reading?
4. **Chills/Rigors:** Any shaking or shivering episodes?
5. **Rash:** Any skin rash or spots? Where? When did it appear?
6. **Headache:** Severity? Location? Neck stiffness?
7. **Respiratory:** Cough? Difficulty breathing? Fast breathing?
8. **Gastrointestinal:** Vomiting? Diarrhea? Abdominal pain?
9. **Urinary:** Burning during urination? Frequent urination?
10. **Travel history:** Recent travel to malaria/dengue areas?
11. **Insect bites:** Any mosquito or tick bites noticed?
12. **Contact:** Anyone else sick at home or village?
13. **Vaccination:** Up to date with immunizations?
14. **Medications:** Any medicines already taken?

## Age-Specific Criteria and Actions

### Infants (<3 Months) - HIGHEST RISK

**ANY fever >100.4°F (38°C) = EMERGENCY**

**Why critical:**
- Immature immune system
- Rapid deterioration possible
- Risk of serious bacterial infection (meningitis, sepsis)
- Cannot communicate symptoms

**Action:** Immediate referral to PHC for ALL fevers in infants <3 months

### Young Children (3 Months - 5 Years)

**Refer to PHC if:**
- Fever >103°F (39.5°C)
- Fever lasting >3 days
- Febrile seizures (convulsions with fever)
- Difficulty breathing or fast breathing
- Not drinking or feeding
- Unusually sleepy or difficult to wake
- Persistent vomiting
- Rash that doesn't fade when pressed

**Febrile Seizures (Common in this age):**
- Usually harmless but frightening
- Classify as EMERGENCY
- Place child on side, protect from injury
- Do NOT put anything in mouth
- Time the seizure duration
- Transport to PHC after seizure stops

### School-Age Children (5-12 Years)

**Refer to PHC if:**
- Fever >103°F (39.5°C) lasting >24 hours
- Fever >3 days
- Severe headache with vomiting
- Neck stiffness
- Difficulty breathing
- Abdominal pain
- Rash
- Decreased activity or alertness

### Adults (12-60 Years)

**Refer to PHC if:**
- Fever >103°F (39.5°C)
- Fever lasting >5 days
- Severe headache with neck stiffness
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- Difficulty breathing
- Chest pain
- Persistent vomiting
- Signs of dehydration
- Rash with fever

### Elderly (>60 Years)

**Lower threshold for referral:**
- May not mount high fever even with serious infection
- Fever >101°F (38.3°C) = refer to PHC
- Watch for confusion (may be only sign of infection)
- Higher risk of complications
- Faster deterioration

### Pregnant Women

**ANY fever = refer to PHC**
- Risk to both mother and fetus
- Some infections can cause miscarriage or birth defects
- Medications need to be carefully selected
- Higher risk of complications

## Danger Signs (EMERGENCY - Immediate Referral)

**Classify as EMERGENCY if ANY of these present:**

**Neurological:**
- Neck stiffness (cannot touch chin to chest)
- Severe headache with vomiting
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- Seizures or convulsions
- Difficulty waking up or extreme lethargy
- Unusual behavior or irritability

**Respiratory:**
- Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
- Fast breathing (see rates below)
- Chest indrawing (ribs pulling in)
- Grunting sounds
- Bluish lips or fingernails

**Circulatory:**
- Rash that doesn't fade when pressed (petechial rash - sign of meningitis)
- Pale, cold, clammy skin
- Weak, rapid pulse
- Low blood pressure
- Mottled skin

**Dehydration:**
- Not drinking or unable to drink
- No urine for >8 hours (adults) or >6 hours (children)
- Sunken eyes
- Very dry mouth and tongue
- Skin pinch returns very slowly

**Other Critical Signs:**
- Temperature >106°F (41°C)
- Persistent vomiting (cannot keep anything down)
- Severe abdominal pain
- Blood in stool or urine
- Jaundice (yellow eyes or skin)
- Infant <3 months with any fever

## Respiratory Rate Reference (For Fast Breathing Assessment)

**Normal Respiratory Rates:**
- Newborn (0-1 month): 30-60 breaths/minute
- Infant (1-12 months): 30-50 breaths/minute
- Toddler (1-3 years): 24-40 breaths/minute
- Preschool (3-6 years): 22-34 breaths/minute
- School age (6-12 years): 18-30 breaths/minute
- Adolescent/Adult: 12-20 breaths/minute

**Fast Breathing (Tachypnea) Criteria:**
- Infant <2 months: >60/minute
- Infant 2-12 months: >50/minute
- Child 1-5 years: >40/minute
- Child >5 years: >30/minute
- Adult: >20/minute

## Common Causes of Fever in Rural India

### 1. Malaria
**Suspect if:**
- High fever with chills and rigors (shaking)
- Fever pattern: comes and goes every 2-3 days
- Headache, body aches
- Nausea, vomiting
- Recent travel to endemic area
- Rainy season or mosquito exposure

**Action:** Refer to PHC for rapid diagnostic test and treatment

### 2. Dengue Fever
**Suspect if:**
- High fever (sudden onset)
- Severe body aches and joint pain ("breakbone fever")
- Headache (especially behind eyes)
- Rash (appears 2-5 days after fever starts)
- Bleeding signs (nose bleeds, gum bleeding, easy bruising)
- Low platelet count if tested

**Danger signs (Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever):**
- Persistent vomiting
- Severe abdominal pain
- Bleeding from any site
- Restlessness or lethargy
- Cold, clammy skin

**Action:** Immediate referral for suspected dengue with danger signs

### 3. Typhoid Fever
**Suspect if:**
- Prolonged fever (>5 days)
- Stepwise increase in fever
- Abdominal pain
- Constipation or diarrhea
- Headache
- Loss of appetite
- Rose spots on abdomen (sometimes)

**Action:** Refer to PHC for Widal test and treatment

### 4. Pneumonia
**Suspect if:**
- Fever with cough
- Fast breathing or difficulty breathing
- Chest pain
- Chest indrawing (children)
- Decreased activity

**Action:** Refer to PHC, especially if fast breathing present

### 5. Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
**Suspect if:**
- Fever with burning during urination
- Frequent urination
- Lower abdominal pain
- Foul-smelling urine
- Back pain (kidney involvement)

**Action:** Refer to PHC for urine test and antibiotics

### 6. Viral Infections (Common Cold, Flu)
**Usually:**
- Low to moderate fever
- Runny nose, sore throat
- Cough
- Body aches
- Usually resolves in 3-5 days

**Action:** Home management unless danger signs or high fever

## Immediate Home Management (While Preparing for Referral)

### 1. Fever Reduction

**Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) - FIRST CHOICE:**
- **Adults:** 500-1000mg every 6 hours (max 4000mg/day)
- **Children:** 10-15mg/kg every 6 hours
- **Infants >3 months:** 60-120mg every 6 hours

**Important:**
- Do NOT give aspirin to children <16 years (risk of Reye's syndrome)
- Do NOT give ibuprofen to infants <6 months
- Do NOT exceed maximum daily dose

### 2. Physical Cooling Methods

**Tepid Sponging:**
- Use lukewarm water (NOT cold or ice water)
- Sponge forehead, neck, armpits, groin
- Do 10-15 minutes at a time
- Stop if shivering occurs (shivering increases temperature)

**Environmental:**
- Light clothing (remove excess layers)
- Cool room (fan or open windows)
- Do NOT bundle up in blankets

**What NOT to do:**
- Do NOT use ice baths (causes shivering, raises core temperature)
- Do NOT use alcohol rubs (absorbed through skin, toxic)
- Do NOT force cold water (causes shivering)

### 3. Hydration (CRITICAL)

**Encourage frequent fluids:**
- Water
- ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution)
- Coconut water
- Thin dal water
- Soup or broth
- Fruit juices (diluted)

**Amounts:**
- **Adults:** 2-3 liters per day
- **Children:** 100-150ml/kg per day
- **Infants:** Continue breastfeeding + extra water

**Signs of adequate hydration:**
- Urinating regularly (light yellow urine)
- Moist mouth and tongue
- Normal skin turgor
- Alert and active

### 4. Nutrition

**During fever:**
- Light, easily digestible foods
- Small, frequent meals
- Continue breastfeeding (infants)
- Do NOT force food if no appetite
- Increase fluids more than solids

### 5. Rest

- Adequate rest and sleep
- Avoid physical exertion
- Stay home from school/work
- Quiet, comfortable environment

## When to Refer to PHC (Summary)

**Immediate (EMERGENCY):**
- Any danger sign present
- Infant <3 months with fever
- Temperature >106°F (41°C)
- Seizures
- Difficulty breathing
- Altered consciousness
- Neck stiffness
- Rash that doesn't fade
- Signs of shock

**Urgent (Within 24 hours):**
- Fever >103°F (39.5°C) not responding to paracetamol
- Fever >3 days in children
- Fever >5 days in adults
- Suspected malaria/dengue/typhoid
- Pregnant woman with fever
- Persistent vomiting
- Signs of dehydration
- Severe pain

**Routine (Within 2-3 days):**
- Low-grade fever with mild symptoms
- Fever responding to paracetamol
- No danger signs
- Good oral intake
- Normal activity level

## Prevention Counseling

**Advise families on:**

**Malaria Prevention:**
- Use mosquito nets (especially at night)
- Wear long sleeves and pants at dusk
- Use mosquito repellent
- Eliminate standing water around home

**Dengue Prevention:**
- Mosquitoes bite during day
- Cover water storage containers
- Empty flower pots, old tires
- Use mosquito repellent during day

**General Hygiene:**
- Hand washing before meals and after toilet
- Safe drinking water (boiled or filtered)
- Food hygiene
- Avoid crowded places during flu season

**Vaccination:**
- Keep children's immunizations up to date
- Annual flu vaccine for high-risk individuals

## Documentation

**Record these details:**
1. Date and time of fever onset
2. Highest temperature recorded
3. Pattern of fever
4. Associated symptoms
5. Medications given (dose and time)
6. Fluid intake
7. Urine output
8. Any danger signs observed
9. Action taken (referral, home management)

## Follow-Up

**For cases managed at home:**
- Recheck temperature every 4-6 hours
- Monitor for danger signs
- Ensure adequate fluid intake
- Return to PHC if:
  - Fever persists >3 days (children) or >5 days (adults)
  - Any danger signs develop
  - Condition worsens
  - Not improving despite treatment

---
**DISCLAIMER:** This is a general triage guideline for ASHA workers. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or treatment. Always refer critical cases to the nearest PHC immediately.
