# Severe Malnutrition Protocol

## Overview

Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM) is a major cause of child mortality in rural India. Early identification and appropriate management can save lives. ASHA workers play a critical role in screening and referral.

## Definition

**Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM) in children 6-59 months:**
- Weight-for-height <-3 SD (Z-score)
- OR Mid-Upper Arm Circumference (MUAC) <11.5 cm
- OR Bilateral pitting edema

---

## SCREENING TOOLS

### 1. MUAC (Mid-Upper Arm Circumference)

**How to measure:**
1. Use MUAC tape (color-coded)
2. Measure left upper arm
3. Find midpoint between shoulder and elbow
4. Wrap tape around arm (not too tight, not too loose)
5. Read measurement

**Interpretation (children 6-59 months):**
- **Green (>13.5 cm):** Normal
- **Yellow (12.5-13.5 cm):** Moderate malnutrition
- **Red (<11.5 cm):** Severe malnutrition - REFER IMMEDIATELY
- **Orange (11.5-12.5 cm):** At risk

### 2. Bilateral Pitting Edema

**How to check:**
1. Press thumb on top of both feet for 3 seconds
2. Remove thumb
3. If indentation remains = pitting edema

**Grading:**
- **+:** Both feet
- **++:** Both feet, lower legs, hands
- **+++:** Generalized edema (face, limbs, body)

**ANY bilateral pitting edema = SAM = REFER IMMEDIATELY**

### 3. Weight-for-Height

- Requires growth chart
- <-3 SD = SAM
- Usually done at health facility

---

## TYPES OF SEVERE MALNUTRITION

### 1. Marasmus

**Characteristics:**
- Severe wasting (very thin)
- "Old man" appearance
- Skin and bones
- No edema
- Alert but irritable
- Loose, wrinkled skin
- Loss of subcutaneous fat

**Cause:** Severe calorie deficiency

### 2. Kwashiorkor

**Characteristics:**
- Bilateral pitting edema (swelling)
- May appear "well-nourished" due to edema
- Skin changes (flaky, peeling)
- Hair changes (thin, sparse, discolored, easily pluckable)
- Enlarged liver
- Apathetic, irritable
- Moon face (facial edema)

**Cause:** Severe protein deficiency

### 3. Marasmic-Kwashiorkor

**Characteristics:**
- Combination of both
- Severe wasting with edema
- Worst prognosis

---

## DANGER SIGNS (Medical Complications)

**Classify as CRITICAL EMERGENCY if ANY present:**

### 1. Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar)
- Lethargy or unconsciousness
- Cold body temperature
- Sweating
- Weak or absent cry

### 2. Hypothermia (Low Body Temperature)
- Body temperature <35.5°C (95.9°F)
- Cold to touch
- Lethargic

### 3. Dehydration
- Sunken eyes
- Very slow skin pinch (>2 seconds)
- Lethargic or unconscious
- Unable to drink

### 4. Severe Infection
- Fever or hypothermia
- Fast breathing
- Severe cough
- Pus discharge
- Skin infections

### 5. Severe Anemia
- Very pale palms
- Fast breathing
- Weak and lethargic

### 6. Other Danger Signs
- Not able to drink or breastfeed
- Vomiting everything
- Recent seizures
- Lethargic or unconscious
- Fast breathing (pneumonia)

---

## ASSESSMENT

### History

**Ask about:**
1. Feeding history:
   - Breastfeeding?
   - Complementary foods?
   - Frequency and amount
   - Food diversity

2. Recent illness:
   - Diarrhea
   - Fever
   - Cough
   - Measles

3. Immunization status

4. Family situation:
   - Food security
   - Number of children
   - Mother's health

### Physical Examination

**Check:**
1. MUAC measurement
2. Bilateral pitting edema
3. Weight (if scale available)
4. Temperature
5. Respiratory rate
6. Skin and hair changes
7. Eye signs (vitamin A deficiency)
8. Alertness and activity

---

## CLASSIFICATION AND MANAGEMENT

### SAM with Complications (EMERGENCY)

**Criteria:**
- SAM (MUAC <11.5 cm or edema) PLUS any danger sign
- OR Edema grade +++

**Management:**
- **Refer IMMEDIATELY** to Nutrition Rehabilitation Center (NRC) or hospital
- Needs inpatient care
- Specialized feeding (F-75, F-100)
- Treatment of complications
- 24-hour medical supervision

### SAM without Complications (URGENT)

**Criteria:**
- SAM (MUAC <11.5 cm or edema + or ++)
- No danger signs
- Good appetite
- Alert and active

**Management:**
- **Refer to Malnutrition Treatment Center (MTC)** or PHC
- May be managed as outpatient
- Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food (RUTF)
- Weekly follow-up
- Treatment of infections

### Moderate Acute Malnutrition (MAM)

**Criteria:**
- MUAC 11.5-12.5 cm (yellow zone)
- No edema

**Management:**
- Supplementary feeding program
- Nutrition counseling
- Weekly monitoring
- Refer if deteriorates

---

## IMMEDIATE ACTIONS (While Preparing for Referral)

### 1. Prevent/Treat Hypoglycemia

**If child is conscious and can drink:**
- Give sugar water or glucose water
- 1 teaspoon sugar in 50ml water
- Give 50ml immediately
- Repeat every 30 minutes until reaching facility

**If unconscious:**
- Do NOT give anything by mouth
- Transport immediately

### 2. Prevent/Treat Hypothermia

- Keep child warm
- Skin-to-skin contact with mother (kangaroo care)
- Cover head
- Warm blankets
- Avoid cold exposure during transport

### 3. Start Feeding (if conscious and can eat)

**If breastfeeding:**
- Continue breastfeeding frequently

**If not breastfeeding:**
- Give small, frequent feeds
- Diluted milk or porridge
- Every 2-3 hours
- Small amounts (50-100ml)

### 4. Give ORS (if dehydrated)

**Special ORS for SAM (ReSoMal):**
- If available, use ReSoMal
- If not, use regular ORS but give slowly
- 5ml/kg every 30 minutes for 2 hours
- Then 5-10ml/kg/hour

**Caution:** Overhydration is dangerous in SAM

### 5. Keep Warm

- Avoid bathing
- Dress warmly
- Keep in warm room
- Skin-to-skin contact

---

## TRANSPORT

**Preparation:**
1. Inform receiving facility
2. Give sugar water before transport
3. Keep child warm
4. Continue breastfeeding if possible
5. Bring mother or caregiver

**During Transport:**
- Monitor consciousness
- Keep warm
- Give sugar water every 30 minutes if conscious
- Watch for danger signs

---

## READY-TO-USE THERAPEUTIC FOOD (RUTF)

### What is RUTF?

- Peanut-based paste
- High energy, high protein
- Contains all essential nutrients
- Does not need cooking or water
- Long shelf life
- Safe for home use

### Common Brands in India

- Peanut-based RUTF sachets
- Distributed through government programs

### Dosage

**Based on weight:**
- 5-7 kg: 1 sachet per day
- 7-10 kg: 1.5 sachets per day
- 10-15 kg: 2 sachets per day
- >15 kg: 2.5-3 sachets per day

**Divide into 6-8 small feeds per day**

### How to Use

1. Open sachet
2. Give directly or mix with small amount of water
3. Give slowly (child may vomit if too fast)
4. Continue breastfeeding
5. Give water separately (not mixed with RUTF)

## PREVENTION

### Infant and Young Child Feeding (IYCF)

**0-6 months:**
- Exclusive breastfeeding
- No water, no other foods
- Breastfeed on demand (8-12 times/day)

**6-8 months:**
- Continue breastfeeding
- Start complementary foods
- 2-3 times per day
- Thick porridge, mashed foods

**9-11 months:**
- Continue breastfeeding
- 3-4 meals per day
- Family foods, chopped or mashed
- 1-2 snacks

**12-24 months:**
- Continue breastfeeding
- 3-4 meals per day
- Family foods
- 1-2 nutritious snacks

### Dietary Diversity

**Include foods from all groups:**
1. Staples (rice, wheat, millets)
2. Pulses and legumes (dal, beans)
3. Dairy (milk, curd, paneer)
4. Eggs, meat, fish
5. Vegetables (especially green leafy)
6. Fruits (especially yellow/orange)
7. Fats and oils

### Micronutrients

**Vitamin A:**
- Green leafy vegetables
- Yellow/orange fruits and vegetables
- Vitamin A supplementation (every 6 months)

**Iron:**
- Meat, eggs, fish
- Green leafy vegetables
- Iron-fortified foods
- Iron supplementation if needed

**Zinc:**
- Meat, eggs, fish
- Pulses and legumes

### Deworming

- Albendazole every 6 months (>1 year)
- Reduces malnutrition

### Immunization

- Complete immunization schedule
- Prevents infections that cause malnutrition

---

## GROWTH MONITORING

### Regular Monitoring

- Weigh child monthly
- Plot on growth chart
- MUAC measurement every month
- Early detection of malnutrition

### Growth Chart Interpretation

**Upward trend:** Good growth
**Flat line:** Growth faltering - investigate
**Downward trend:** Losing weight - urgent action needed

---

## COMMON CAUSES OF MALNUTRITION

### 1. Inadequate Food Intake
- Poverty
- Food insecurity
- Poor feeding practices
- Early weaning

### 2. Repeated Infections
- Diarrhea
- Pneumonia
- Measles
- Tuberculosis
- Worm infestations

### 3. Poor Care Practices
- Inadequate breastfeeding
- Late introduction of complementary foods
- Thin, watery porridge
- Infrequent feeding

### 4. Underlying Conditions
- HIV/AIDS
- Tuberculosis
- Congenital heart disease
- Chronic diarrhea

---

## FOLLOW-UP

### Weekly Monitoring (for SAM)

**Check:**
1. Weight gain (should gain 5-10g/kg/day)
2. MUAC measurement
3. Edema (should reduce)
4. Appetite
5. Any complications

**Refer back to facility if:**
- Not gaining weight
- Developing complications
- Edema increasing
- Poor appetite

### Discharge Criteria

**From inpatient care:**
- No edema for 2 weeks
- MUAC >11.5 cm
- Weight-for-height >-3 SD
- Good appetite
- No medical complications

**Continue follow-up** for 6 months to prevent relapse

---

## COUNSELING FOR MOTHERS

### Key Messages

1. **Breastfeeding:**
   - Exclusive for 6 months
   - Continue up to 2 years or beyond

2. **Complementary Feeding:**
   - Start at 6 months
   - Thick, energy-dense foods
   - Frequent feeding (5-6 times/day)
   - Variety of foods

3. **Hygiene:**
   - Handwashing before feeding
   - Clean utensils
   - Safe water
   - Food hygiene

4. **Illness Management:**
   - Continue feeding during illness
   - Increase feeding after illness
   - Seek care early for infections

5. **Growth Monitoring:**
   - Regular weighing
   - Attend growth monitoring sessions

---

## RED FLAGS (Immediate Referral)

- MUAC <11.5 cm (red zone)
- Any bilateral pitting edema
- Lethargic or unconscious
- Not able to drink or breastfeed
- Vomiting everything
- Seizures
- Very pale
- Fast breathing
- High fever or hypothermia

---

**DISCLAIMER:** This is a general triage guideline for ASHA workers. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or treatment. Always refer critical cases to the nearest PHC immediately.
