# Poisoning Management Protocol

## Overview

Poisoning is a common emergency in rural India, especially pesticide poisoning, kerosene ingestion in children, and rat poison. Quick recognition and appropriate first aid can save lives.

## General Principles

**The 4 Ps of Poisoning:**
1. **Poison:** What was ingested/exposed?
2. **Person:** Who is affected? (age, weight)
3. **Period:** When did exposure occur?
4. **Path:** How did poison enter? (ingestion, inhalation, skin, injection)

## 1. ORGANOPHOSPHATE PESTICIDE POISONING

### Overview

Most common and dangerous poisoning in rural areas. Used in agriculture. Highly toxic.

### Common Products

- Malathion
- Parathion
- Chlorpyrifos
- Monocrotophos
- Dichlorvos

### Signs and Symptoms (SLUDGE Syndrome)

**S - Salivation:** Excessive drooling
**L - Lacrimation:** Excessive tearing
**U - Urination:** Involuntary urination
**D - Defecation:** Diarrhea, involuntary bowel movements
**G - GI upset:** Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps
**E - Emesis:** Vomiting

**Additional symptoms:**
- Pinpoint pupils (miosis)
- Excessive sweating
- Muscle twitching and weakness
- Difficulty breathing
- Confusion or unconsciousness
- Seizures
- Slow heart rate
- Garlic-like odor on breath

### Emergency Criteria

**ALL pesticide poisoning = CRITICAL EMERGENCY**

### Immediate Actions

1. **Protect yourself:**
   - Wear gloves if available
   - Avoid contact with contaminated clothing
   - Work in well-ventilated area

2. **Remove from exposure:**
   - Remove contaminated clothing
   - Wash skin thoroughly with soap and water
   - Wash eyes if exposed

3. **Airway and breathing:**
   - Ensure airway is clear
   - Position on side if vomiting
   - Give oxygen if available

4. **Do NOT induce vomiting** (can cause aspiration)

5. **If conscious and can swallow:**
   - Give activated charcoal if available (within 1 hour of ingestion)
   - Dose: 1g/kg body weight mixed with water

6. **Transport IMMEDIATELY** to PHC

7. **Bring poison container** or note name of pesticide

### Antidote

**Atropine** - Available at PHC
- Reverses SLUDGE symptoms
- Given by injection
- May need repeated doses

**Pralidoxime (PAM)** - Also given at PHC
- Reverses muscle weakness
- Most effective if given early

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT induce vomiting
- Do NOT give milk or oil (increases absorption)
- Do NOT delay transport
- Do NOT contaminate yourself

## 2. KEROSENE POISONING

### Overview

Common in children who accidentally drink kerosene stored in drink bottles. Very dangerous if aspirated into lungs.

### Signs and Symptoms

**Immediate:**
- Coughing and choking
- Burning sensation in mouth and throat
- Vomiting
- Kerosene smell on breath

**Later (if aspirated into lungs):**
- Difficulty breathing
- Rapid breathing
- Chest pain
- Fever
- Cyanosis (blue lips)
- Pneumonia (chemical pneumonitis)

### Emergency Criteria

**ALL kerosene ingestion = EMERGENCY**

### Immediate Actions

1. **Do NOT induce vomiting** (high risk of aspiration)

2. **Do NOT give anything by mouth**

3. **If vomiting occurs:**
   - Position on side
   - Prevent aspiration

4. **Monitor breathing** closely

5. **Transport to PHC immediately**

6. **Bring kerosene container**

### What NOT to Do

- **NEVER induce vomiting** (most important!)
- Do NOT give milk or oil
- Do NOT give activated charcoal (not effective for kerosene)

### Prevention Counseling

- Never store kerosene in drink bottles
- Keep kerosene in labeled containers
- Store out of children's reach
- Use child-proof containers

## 3. RAT POISON (RODENTICIDE) POISONING

### Types

**Warfarin-type (anticoagulants):**
- Causes bleeding
- Symptoms delayed (2-5 days)

**Zinc phosphide:**
- Releases toxic gas in stomach
- Rapid onset

**Yellow phosphorus:**
- Causes liver damage
- Garlic odor

### Signs and Symptoms

**Warfarin-type:**
- Bleeding from gums, nose
- Blood in urine or stool
- Easy bruising
- Vomiting blood
- Symptoms appear 2-5 days after ingestion

**Zinc phosphide:**
- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain
- Garlic breath odor
- Difficulty breathing
- Shock

### Emergency Criteria

**ALL rat poison ingestion = EMERGENCY**

### Immediate Actions

1. **Identify poison type** (bring container)

2. **If recent ingestion (<1 hour) and patient conscious:**
   - Give activated charcoal if available

3. **For warfarin-type:**
   - May not show symptoms immediately
   - Still refer to PHC for monitoring and Vitamin K

4. **For zinc phosphide:**
   - Do NOT induce vomiting (releases toxic gas)
   - Transport immediately

5. **Monitor for bleeding**

6. **Transport to PHC**

### Antidote

**Vitamin K** - For warfarin-type poisoning
- Given at PHC
- May need for several weeks

## 4. ALCOHOL POISONING

### Overview

Includes ethanol (drinking alcohol) and methanol (industrial alcohol, sometimes mixed in illicit liquor).

### Ethanol (Drinking Alcohol) Poisoning

**Signs:**
- Confusion or stupor
- Vomiting
- Slow or irregular breathing
- Low body temperature
- Pale or bluish skin
- Unconsciousness

**Actions:**
1. Position on side (prevent aspiration)
2. Keep warm
3. Monitor breathing
4. Transport to PHC if severe

### Methanol Poisoning (CRITICAL)

**Sources:**
- Industrial alcohol
- Contaminated illicit liquor
- Antifreeze

**Signs (appear 12-24 hours after ingestion):**
- Severe abdominal pain
- Nausea and vomiting
- Blurred vision or blindness
- Difficulty breathing
- Seizures
- Unconsciousness

**Emergency Criteria:**
**ALL suspected methanol poisoning = CRITICAL EMERGENCY**

**Actions:**
1. **Give ethanol** (drinking alcohol) if available and patient conscious
   - Ethanol competes with methanol, reduces toxicity
   - Give 50ml of whisky or similar
2. **Transport IMMEDIATELY** to hospital
3. **Antidote:** Fomepizole or ethanol IV at hospital

## 6. PLANT AND MUSHROOM POISONING

### Common Poisonous Plants in India

- Datura (Jimson weed)
- Oleander
- Castor beans
- Abrus seeds (rosary pea)

### Signs (vary by plant)

- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain
- Diarrhea
- Confusion or hallucinations
- Seizures
- Cardiac arrhythmias

### Actions

1. **Identify plant** if possible (bring sample)
2. **If recent ingestion:** Give activated charcoal
3. **Transport to PHC**
4. **Supportive care**

## 7. DRUG OVERDOSE

### Common Drugs

- Sleeping pills (benzodiazepines)
- Pain medications (opioids)
- Antidepressants
- Paracetamol

### Signs

- Drowsiness or unconsciousness
- Slow breathing
- Pinpoint or dilated pupils
- Confusion
- Seizures

### Actions

1. **Identify drug** (bring container)
2. **If conscious:** Give activated charcoal (if <1 hour)
3. **If unconscious:** Position on side
4. **Monitor breathing**
5. **Transport to PHC**

### Paracetamol Overdose (Special Case)

- May not show symptoms initially
- Causes liver damage after 24-48 hours
- **Antidote:** N-acetylcysteine (NAC) - most effective if given within 8 hours
- **ALL paracetamol overdoses need hospital evaluation**

## GENERAL POISONING MANAGEMENT

### Initial Assessment

**Ask these questions:**
1. What was the poison?
2. How much was taken?
3. When did exposure occur?
4. How did it enter body? (swallowed, inhaled, skin contact)
5. Is patient vomiting?
6. Any symptoms?
7. Any treatment already given?

### ABC Assessment

**A - Airway:**
- Is airway clear?
- Remove any pills or substances from mouth

**B - Breathing:**
- Is patient breathing?
- Respiratory rate and effort

**C - Circulation:**
- Pulse rate and strength
- Signs of shock

**D - Disability:**
- Level of consciousness
- Pupil size

### Decontamination

**Skin exposure:**
- Remove contaminated clothing
- Wash with soap and water for 15-20 minutes
- Protect yourself from contamination

**Eye exposure:**
- Flush with clean water for 15-20 minutes
- Hold eyelids open
- Flush from inner to outer corner

**Ingestion:**
- Do NOT routinely induce vomiting
- Give activated charcoal if:
  - <1 hour since ingestion
  - Patient is conscious and can swallow
  - Not contraindicated (see below)

### Activated Charcoal

**Dose:** 1g/kg body weight (usually 25-50g for adults)
**Mix with water** to make slurry
**Give within 1 hour** of ingestion for best effect

**Do NOT give if:**
- Unconscious or drowsy
- Seizures
- Corrosive poison (acid, alkali)
- Petroleum products (kerosene, petrol)
- Alcohol
- Iron or lithium

### When to Induce Vomiting

**Generally NOT recommended** - risks outweigh benefits

**NEVER induce vomiting if:**
- Unconscious or drowsy
- Seizures
- Corrosive substances (acids, alkalis, bleach)
- Petroleum products (kerosene, petrol, diesel)
- Sharp objects swallowed
- <6 months old

### Transport

**All poisoning cases should be referred to PHC**

**Bring with you:**
- Poison container or sample
- Vomitus (if present)
- Any medications patient takes
- Information about timing and amount

**During transport:**
- Position on side if vomiting
- Monitor breathing and consciousness
- Keep warm
- Reassure patient

## SPECIFIC ANTIDOTES (Available at Hospital)

| Poison | Antidote |
|--------|----------|
| Organophosphates | Atropine, Pralidoxime (PAM) |
| Warfarin | Vitamin K |
| Paracetamol | N-acetylcysteine (NAC) |
| Opioids | Naloxone |
| Benzodiazepines | Flumazenil |
| Methanol | Fomepizole or Ethanol |
| Snake venom | Anti-Snake Venom (ASV) |

## PREVENTION

### Storage Safety

- Keep all poisons in original labeled containers
- Store out of reach of children
- Use child-proof containers
- Never store in food or drink containers
- Keep pesticides locked away

### Usage Safety

- Read labels before use
- Use protective equipment
- Wash hands after handling
- Don't eat, drink, or smoke while using pesticides
- Dispose of empty containers safely

### Community Education

- Teach safe storage practices
- Warn about dangers of illicit alcohol
- Promote safe pesticide use
- Encourage seeking help early

---

## DANGER SIGNS (Immediate Referral)

- Unconsciousness or drowsiness
- Difficulty breathing
- Seizures
- Severe vomiting or diarrhea
- Abnormal heart rate
- Confusion or hallucinations
- Pinpoint or dilated pupils
- Excessive salivation or sweating
- Muscle weakness or twitching

---

**DISCLAIMER:** This is a general triage guideline for ASHA workers. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or treatment. Always refer critical cases to the nearest PHC immediately.
