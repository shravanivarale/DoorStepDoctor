# Chest Pain (Suspected Cardiac)

## Warning Signs of Heart Attack (Myocardial Infarction)

**Classic Symptoms:**
- **Chest pain or pressure:**
  - Described as crushing, squeezing, heavy, or tight
  - Like "elephant sitting on chest"
  - Lasts >5 minutes or comes and goes
  - Not relieved by rest or position change

**Pain Radiation Patterns:**
- Left arm (most common)
- Right arm
- Both arms
- Jaw or teeth
- Back (between shoulder blades)
- Neck
- Upper abdomen (can mimic indigestion)

**Associated Symptoms:**
- Profuse sweating (cold, clammy sweat)
- Shortness of breath or difficulty breathing
- Nausea or vomiting
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Extreme fatigue or weakness
- Anxiety or feeling of impending doom
- Pale or grayish skin color

## Risk Factors (Higher Suspicion if Present)

**Major Risk Factors:**
- Age >40 years (men), >50 years (women)
- History of diabetes mellitus
- High blood pressure (hypertension)
- High cholesterol
- Current or past smoking
- Family history of heart disease (especially if young age)
- Previous heart attack or angina
- Obesity
- Sedentary lifestyle

**Immediate Risk Factors:**
- Recent physical exertion
- Emotional stress
- Heavy meal
- Cold weather exposure

## Gender Differences in Symptoms

**Men typically have:**
- Classic crushing chest pain
- Pain radiating to left arm
- Sweating

**Women may have atypical symptoms:**
- Milder chest discomfort (may be ignored)
- More nausea and vomiting
- Back or jaw pain without chest pain
- Extreme fatigue
- Shortness of breath without chest pain
- Dizziness

**Important:** Women's symptoms are often missed - maintain high suspicion!

## Immediate Actions (Critical Time-Sensitive Steps)

**1. Position Patient (FIRST PRIORITY):**
- Make patient sit or lie down in semi-reclined position (45-degree angle)
- Do NOT let patient stand, walk, or exert in any way
- Loosen tight clothing around neck and chest
- Keep patient calm and still

**2. Give Aspirin (If Available and No Contraindications):**
- **Dose:** 300mg (or 4 tablets of 75mg each)
- **Method:** CHEW, do not swallow whole (faster absorption)
- **Give ONLY if:**
  - No known allergy to aspirin
  - No active bleeding or recent bleeding
  - Not already taking blood thinners (warfarin, etc.)
  - No history of stomach ulcers (active)
  - Patient is conscious and can swallow

**3. Monitor Vital Signs:**
- Check pulse every 5 minutes (note rate and strength)
- Observe breathing (rate and difficulty)
- Watch level of consciousness
- Note skin color and temperature

**4. Prepare for Emergency:**
- Call 108 ambulance immediately
- Have someone stay with patient continuously
- Be prepared to perform CPR if needed
- Gather patient's medications if available

**5. Reassure Patient:**
- Speak calmly and confidently
- Explain that help is coming
- Reduce anxiety (stress worsens heart attack)
- Keep environment quiet

## Emergency Criteria (Refer IMMEDIATELY - Every Minute Counts!)

**ALL suspected heart attacks require IMMEDIATE transport:**

**Classify as CRITICAL EMERGENCY if:**
- Chest pain lasting >5 minutes
- Chest pain with any of: sweating, shortness of breath, nausea, dizziness
- First-time chest pain in person with risk factors
- Pain not relieved by rest
- Pain worsening or recurring
- Loss of consciousness
- Severe difficulty breathing
- Irregular or very fast/slow pulse
- Signs of shock (pale, cold, weak pulse)

**Time is Muscle:** Every minute of delay = more heart muscle damage!

**Golden Hour:** First 60 minutes are critical for treatment effectiveness

## Differential Diagnosis (Other Causes of Chest Pain)

**When to suspect NON-cardiac causes:**

**Musculoskeletal (muscle/bone pain):**
- Pain worsens with movement or deep breathing
- Tender to touch on chest wall
- Recent injury or strain
- Sharp, stabbing pain (not pressure)

**Gastric (stomach-related):**
- Burning sensation
- Related to meals
- Relieved by antacids
- Associated with belching or heartburn

**Anxiety/Panic Attack:**
- Young patient with no risk factors
- Associated with hyperventilation
- Tingling in hands/feet
- Feeling of unreality

**However:** When in doubt, ALWAYS treat as cardiac emergency! Better safe than sorry.

## What NOT to Do (Critical Mistakes to Avoid)

**NEVER:**
- Let patient walk or exert themselves (increases heart workload)
- Give food or water (risk of vomiting, aspiration)
- Wait to see if pain improves (time = muscle damage)
- Transport in sitting position without support
- Give aspirin if patient is unconscious or has bleeding
- Delay calling ambulance to "try home remedies"
- Leave patient alone
- Allow patient to drive themselves

## Transport Instructions

**Ambulance Transport (PREFERRED):**
- Call 108 immediately
- Specify "suspected heart attack"
- Give clear location and landmarks
- Have someone wait at roadside to guide ambulance

**If Ambulance Not Available:**
- Use private vehicle ONLY if no other option
- Patient must lie down or semi-recline (NOT sit upright)
- Drive smoothly, avoid jerks
- Have someone monitor patient continuously
- Go to nearest facility with cardiac care

**During Transport:**
- Keep patient calm and reassured
- Monitor breathing and pulse every 5 minutes
- Be prepared for:
  - Loss of consciousness
  - Cardiac arrest (need CPR)
  - Vomiting
- Note time of symptom onset and aspirin given

## CPR Readiness (If Patient Becomes Unconscious)

**Be prepared to start CPR if:**
- Patient becomes unconscious
- No breathing or gasping only
- No pulse felt

**Basic CPR Steps:**
1. Call for help
2. Place patient flat on firm surface
3. Start chest compressions:
   - Center of chest, between nipples
   - Push hard and fast (100-120 per minute)
   - Depth: 2 inches (5cm)
   - Allow full chest recoil between compressions
4. Continue until help arrives or patient recovers

## Information to Collect (For Hospital)

**Important details to note:**
1. Exact time symptoms started
2. Description of pain (location, quality, radiation)
3. What patient was doing when pain started
4. Previous similar episodes
5. Current medications
6. Known medical conditions
7. Allergies
8. Time aspirin was given (if given)
9. Vital signs during transport

## Special Populations

**Diabetic Patients:**
- May have "silent" heart attacks (minimal pain)
- Watch for unusual fatigue, weakness, or confusion
- Higher risk of complications
- Lower threshold for referral

**Elderly Patients:**
- May have atypical symptoms
- Confusion or weakness may be only sign
- Higher mortality risk
- Faster deterioration

**Young Patients (<40 years):**
- Less likely to be cardiac, but still possible
- Consider cocaine use, congenital heart disease
- Don't dismiss symptoms due to age

## Prevention Counseling (For Future)

**After emergency is managed, advise:**
- Regular blood pressure and blood sugar checks
- Smoking cessation
- Healthy diet (less oil, salt, sugar)
- Regular physical activity
- Stress management
- Regular medications if prescribed
- Know warning signs for future

## Red Flags (Extremely Serious)

**Call for immediate help if patient has:**
- Sudden collapse or unconsciousness
- No pulse or breathing
- Severe difficulty breathing (gasping)
- Chest pain with very fast or very slow heart rate
- Chest pain with confusion or altered mental status
- Bluish lips or fingernails (cyanosis)
