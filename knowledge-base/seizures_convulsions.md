# Seizures and Convulsions Protocol

## Overview

Seizures (also called convulsions or fits) are sudden, uncontrolled electrical disturbances in the brain. They can be frightening but most are brief and self-limiting. Knowing how to respond can prevent injury and save lives.

## Types of Seizures

### 1. Generalized Tonic-Clonic Seizures (Grand Mal)
- Loss of consciousness
- Body stiffening (tonic phase)
- Jerking movements (clonic phase)
- May bite tongue
- May lose bladder/bowel control
- Usually lasts 1-3 minutes

### 2. Absence Seizures (Petit Mal)
- Brief loss of awareness (10-20 seconds)
- Staring spell
- No falling or jerking
- Common in children
- May be mistaken for daydreaming

### 3. Focal (Partial) Seizures
- Affects one part of brain
- May have jerking in one limb
- May have strange sensations
- May or may not lose consciousness

### 4. Febrile Seizures
- Occur with high fever in children
- Usually age 6 months to 5 years
- Brief (<5 minutes)
- Usually harmless but frightening

## FEBRILE SEIZURES (Most Common in Rural Practice)

### Overview

Seizures triggered by fever in young children. Occur in 2-5% of children. Usually harmless but require assessment.

### Risk Factors

- Age 6 months to 5 years
- Family history of febrile seizures
- Rapid rise in temperature
- Temperature >100.4°F (38°C)

### Signs

- Sudden loss of consciousness
- Body stiffening
- Jerking movements of arms and legs
- Eyes rolling back
- May turn blue briefly
- Usually lasts <5 minutes

### Simple vs Complex Febrile Seizures

**Simple (90% of cases):**
- Lasts <15 minutes
- Generalized (whole body)
- Doesn't recur within 24 hours
- Child recovers quickly

**Complex (10% of cases):**
- Lasts >15 minutes
- Focal (one side of body)
- Recurs within 24 hours
- Prolonged recovery

### Emergency Criteria

**Classify as EMERGENCY if:**
- First febrile seizure ever
- Seizure lasting >5 minutes
- Multiple seizures in 24 hours
- Child <6 months or >5 years
- Not recovering between seizures
- Neck stiffness (possible meningitis)
- Prolonged drowsiness after seizure

**Classify as URGENT if:**
- Simple febrile seizure (brief, recovered)
- Previous history of simple febrile seizures
- Child alert and active after seizure

### Immediate Actions During Seizure

1. **Stay calm** - most febrile seizures stop on their own

2. **Protect from injury:**
   - Place on side (recovery position)
   - Remove nearby objects
   - Cushion head with soft material
   - Loosen tight clothing

3. **Do NOT:**
   - Put anything in mouth
   - Try to hold child down
   - Give anything by mouth
   - Try to stop the jerking

4. **Time the seizure** - note start and end time

5. **After seizure stops:**
   - Keep on side
   - Check breathing
   - Give fever medication (paracetamol) when fully awake
   - Tepid sponging to reduce fever

### When to Refer

**Immediate referral:**
- First febrile seizure
- Seizure >5 minutes
- Multiple seizures
- Not recovering normally
- Age <6 months or >5 years

**Routine referral (next day):**
- Simple febrile seizure, child recovered
- For evaluation and parent education

### Prevention

- Control fever promptly with paracetamol
- Tepid sponging
- Keep child hydrated
- Remove excess clothing

### Parent Education

- Febrile seizures are usually harmless
- Do not cause brain damage
- Do not cause epilepsy (in most cases)
- May recur with future fevers (30% chance)
- Teach seizure first aid

## EPILEPSY (Recurrent Seizures Without Fever)

### Overview

Chronic condition with recurrent seizures. May be known diagnosis or first presentation.

### Causes

- Idiopathic (unknown cause) - most common
- Birth injury or brain damage
- Head trauma
- Brain infection (meningitis, encephalitis)
- Brain tumor (rare)
- Genetic factors

### Triggers

- Missed medications
- Sleep deprivation
- Stress
- Flashing lights (photosensitive epilepsy)
- Alcohol or drug use
- Illness or fever

### Emergency Criteria

**Classify as EMERGENCY if:**
- Status epilepticus (seizure >5 minutes or multiple seizures without recovery)
- First-time seizure
- Injury during seizure
- Difficulty breathing after seizure
- Seizure in water
- Pregnant woman
- Diabetic patient
- Not recovering normally

**Routine management if:**
- Known epileptic
- Brief seizure (<5 minutes)
- Recovering normally
- No injury

### Immediate Actions During Seizure

1. **Protect from injury:**
   - Clear area of dangerous objects
   - Cushion head
   - Do NOT restrain
   - Loosen tight clothing around neck

2. **Position:**
   - Turn on side (recovery position)
   - Allows saliva to drain
   - Prevents tongue from blocking airway

3. **Time the seizure** - important for treatment decisions

4. **Do NOT:**
   - Put anything in mouth (can break teeth, cause choking)
   - Try to hold tongue
   - Give anything by mouth during seizure
   - Throw water on face

5. **After seizure:**
   - Keep on side until fully awake
   - Check for injuries
   - Allow to rest
   - Reassure and comfort

### Status Epilepticus (CRITICAL EMERGENCY)

**Definition:** Seizure lasting >5 minutes OR multiple seizures without regaining consciousness

**Why critical:**
- Can cause brain damage
- Can be fatal
- Needs immediate medication

**Actions:**
1. **Call for emergency help immediately**
2. **Protect airway** - position on side
3. **Give oxygen** if available
4. **If trained and medication available:**
   - Rectal diazepam or midazolam
5. **Transport immediately** to PHC
6. **Monitor breathing** continuously

## FIRST-TIME SEIZURES (New Onset)

### Possible Causes

**In Children:**
- Febrile seizure
- Meningitis or encephalitis
- Head injury
- Poisoning
- Metabolic disorders

**In Adults:**
- Stroke
- Head injury
- Brain tumor
- Infection (meningitis, encephalitis)
- Alcohol withdrawal
- Drug overdose
- Metabolic disorders (low blood sugar, low sodium)

### Emergency Criteria

**ALL first-time seizures = EMERGENCY**

### Assessment

**Ask about:**
- What was person doing before seizure?
- Any warning signs?
- Description of seizure
- Duration
- Any fever?
- Recent head injury?
- Any medications or drugs?
- Medical history (diabetes, hypertension)

**Check for:**
- Fever
- Neck stiffness (meningitis)
- Head injury
- Signs of stroke
- Blood sugar (if glucometer available)

### Actions

1. **Manage seizure** as described above
2. **Assess for underlying cause**
3. **Check vital signs**
4. **Refer to PHC** for evaluation

## SEIZURES IN SPECIAL POPULATIONS

### Pregnant Women (Eclampsia)

**See obstetric_eclampsia.md for detailed protocol**

**Key points:**
- Seizures in pregnancy = CRITICAL EMERGENCY
- May indicate eclampsia
- Risk to mother and baby
- Needs immediate magnesium sulfate and delivery

### Diabetic Patients

**Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) can cause seizures**

**Actions:**
1. Check blood sugar if possible
2. If low (<70 mg/dL) and patient conscious after seizure:
   - Give sugar water or glucose
3. If unconscious:
   - Do NOT give anything by mouth
   - Transport to PHC for IV glucose

### Elderly Patients

**Higher risk of:**
- Stroke causing seizures
- Medication interactions
- Falls and injuries during seizure

**Lower threshold for hospital referral**

## POST-SEIZURE CARE (Postictal Period)

### What to Expect

- Confusion or disorientation (10-30 minutes)
- Drowsiness or deep sleep
- Headache
- Muscle aches
- Weakness
- May not remember seizure

### Care

1. **Allow to rest** in safe, comfortable position
2. **Monitor breathing** and consciousness
3. **Check for injuries** (tongue bite, head injury, fractures)
4. **Reassure** - person may be confused or frightened
5. **Do NOT give food or drink** until fully alert
6. **Stay with person** until fully recovered

### When to Seek Help

- Not recovering after 30 minutes
- Second seizure occurs
- Difficulty breathing
- Injury during seizure
- First-time seizure
- Pregnant woman
- Seizure in water

## SEIZURE FIRST AID (Quick Reference)

**DO:**
- Stay calm
- Protect from injury
- Turn on side
- Time the seizure
- Stay with person
- Reassure after seizure

**DON'T:**
- Put anything in mouth
- Hold person down
- Give anything by mouth during seizure
- Throw water on face
- Leave person alone

## WHEN TO CALL AMBULANCE (108)

**Call immediately if:**
- Seizure lasts >5 minutes
- Multiple seizures without recovery
- First-time seizure
- Difficulty breathing
- Seizure in water
- Pregnant woman
- Injury during seizure
- Person has diabetes
- Seizure followed by another seizure

## PREVENTION AND MANAGEMENT (For Known Epileptics)

### Medication Compliance

- Take medications regularly
- Do NOT stop suddenly (can trigger seizures)
- Keep medications accessible
- Refill before running out

### Lifestyle Modifications

- Regular sleep schedule
- Avoid alcohol and drugs
- Manage stress
- Avoid known triggers
- Wear medical alert bracelet

### Safety Precautions

- Avoid swimming alone
- Avoid heights
- Avoid operating machinery
- Inform family and friends about condition
- Have seizure action plan

### Regular Follow-Up

- Regular doctor visits
- Medication level checks
- Adjust medications as needed

## MYTHS AND FACTS

**Myth:** Put something in mouth to prevent tongue swallowing
**Fact:** Cannot swallow tongue. Putting objects in mouth can cause injury

**Myth:** Hold person down to stop seizure
**Fact:** Cannot stop seizure by restraining. May cause injury

**Myth:** Throw water on face
**Fact:** Does not help and may cause aspiration

**Myth:** Epilepsy is contagious
**Fact:** Not contagious. Not caused by evil spirits

**Myth:** People with epilepsy cannot lead normal lives
**Fact:** With proper treatment, most can lead normal, productive lives

## COMMUNITY EDUCATION

### Reduce Stigma

- Epilepsy is a medical condition, not a curse
- People with epilepsy can work, marry, have children
- Seizures can be controlled with medication
- Educate community about seizure first aid

### Seizure First Aid Training

- Teach family members
- Train school teachers
- Community awareness programs
