# Vital Signs Reference Guide

## Overview

Vital signs are measurements of the body's basic functions. They help assess a person's health status and identify emergencies. This guide provides normal ranges by age and instructions for measurement.

---

## 1. RESPIRATORY RATE (Breaths Per Minute)

### Normal Ranges by Age

| Age Group | Normal Range | Fast Breathing (Tachypnea) |
|-----------|--------------|----------------------------|
| Newborn (0-1 month) | 30-60 | >60 |
| Infant (1-12 months) | 30-50 | >50 |
| Toddler (1-3 years) | 24-40 | >40 |
| Preschool (3-6 years) | 22-34 | >34 |
| School age (6-12 years) | 18-30 | >30 |
| Adolescent (12-18 years) | 12-20 | >20 |
| Adult (>18 years) | 12-20 | >20 |

### How to Measure

1. **Observe chest movements** (one rise and fall = one breath)
2. **Count for 30 seconds** and multiply by 2
3. **Or count for full 60 seconds** for more accuracy
4. **Do not tell patient** you're counting (they may alter breathing)
5. **Count when patient is calm** (not crying or active)

### Interpretation

**Tachypnea (Fast Breathing):**
- Pneumonia
- Asthma
- Heart failure
- Fever
- Anxiety
- Pain

**Bradypnea (Slow Breathing):**
- Drug overdose
- Head injury
- Hypothermia

**Irregular Breathing:**
- Serious brain or lung problem
- Needs immediate attention

### Danger Signs

- Respiratory rate >30 in adults
- Respiratory rate >50 in infants
- Respiratory rate >40 in children
- Chest indrawing (ribs pulling in)
- Grunting sounds
- Nasal flaring
- Bluish lips or fingernails (cyanosis)

## 2. HEART RATE / PULSE (Beats Per Minute)

### Normal Ranges by Age

| Age Group | Normal Range | Slow (Bradycardia) | Fast (Tachycardia) |
|-----------|--------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Newborn (0-1 month) | 120-160 | <120 | >160 |
| Infant (1-12 months) | 100-150 | <100 | >150 |
| Toddler (1-3 years) | 90-140 | <90 | >140 |
| Preschool (3-6 years) | 80-120 | <80 | >120 |
| School age (6-12 years) | 70-110 | <70 | >110 |
| Adolescent (12-18 years) | 60-100 | <60 | >100 |
| Adult (>18 years) | 60-100 | <60 | >100 |

### How to Measure

**Radial Pulse (Wrist):**
1. Place 2-3 fingers on inside of wrist, below thumb
2. Feel for pulsation
3. Count for 30 seconds and multiply by 2
4. Or count for full 60 seconds

**Carotid Pulse (Neck):**
1. Place 2 fingers on side of neck, beside windpipe
2. Do NOT press both sides simultaneously
3. Count as above

**Brachial Pulse (Arm):**
1. Used in infants
2. Inside of upper arm, between elbow and shoulder

**Femoral Pulse (Groin):**
1. Used in emergencies
2. Midpoint of groin crease

### Pulse Characteristics to Note

**Rate:** Beats per minute
**Rhythm:** Regular or irregular
**Strength:** Strong, weak, or thready
**Equality:** Same on both sides

### Interpretation

**Tachycardia (Fast Heart Rate):**
- Fever
- Dehydration
- Shock
- Heart problems
- Anxiety
- Pain
- Anemia

**Bradycardia (Slow Heart Rate):**
- Heart block
- Hypothermia
- Drug overdose
- Athletes (normal for them)

**Irregular Pulse:**
- Heart arrhythmia
- Needs medical evaluation

**Weak/Thready Pulse:**
- Shock
- Severe dehydration
- Heart failure
- Emergency situation

## 3. BLOOD PRESSURE (mmHg)

### Normal Ranges by Age

| Age Group | Normal Systolic | Normal Diastolic | Hypertension |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------|--------------|
| Children 1-3 years | 80-110 | 50-70 | >110/70 |
| Children 3-6 years | 90-120 | 55-75 | >120/75 |
| Children 6-12 years | 95-125 | 60-80 | >125/80 |
| Adolescent (12-18 years) | 100-135 | 65-85 | >135/85 |
| Adult (18-60 years) | 90-140 | 60-90 | >140/90 |
| Elderly (>60 years) | 90-150 | 60-90 | >150/90 |

### How to Measure (If BP Apparatus Available)

1. **Patient should be:**
   - Seated or lying down
   - Rested for 5 minutes
   - Arm supported at heart level
   - Not talking

2. **Wrap cuff** around upper arm (2-3 cm above elbow)

3. **Inflate cuff** until pulse disappears

4. **Slowly deflate** while listening with stethoscope

5. **Note readings:**
   - Systolic: First sound heard
   - Diastolic: Sound disappears

### Interpretation

**Hypotension (Low Blood Pressure):**
- Systolic <90 mmHg
- Causes: Shock, dehydration, bleeding, heart problems
- Symptoms: Dizziness, weakness, fainting

**Hypertension (High Blood Pressure):**
- >140/90 mmHg in adults
- May have no symptoms
- Can cause stroke, heart attack, kidney damage

**Hypertensive Crisis (EMERGENCY):**
- >180/120 mmHg
- Severe headache, chest pain, vision changes
- Immediate referral needed

## 4. TEMPERATURE

### Normal Ranges

| Measurement Site | Normal Range | Fever |
|------------------|--------------|-------|
| Oral | 97-99°F (36-37.2°C) | >100.4°F (38°C) |
| Axillary (Armpit) | 96.6-98.6°F (35.9-37°C) | >99.5°F (37.5°C) |
| Rectal | 98.6-100.4°F (37-38°C) | >100.4°F (38°C) |
| Tympanic (Ear) | 97.5-99.5°F (36.4-37.5°C) | >100.4°F (38°C) |

### How to Measure

**Axillary (Armpit) - Most Common in Field:**
1. Place thermometer in dry armpit
2. Hold arm close to body
3. Wait 3-5 minutes (mercury) or until beep (digital)
4. Read temperature
5. Add 1°F to get approximate oral equivalent

**Oral:**
1. Place under tongue
2. Keep mouth closed
3. Wait 3 minutes (mercury) or until beep (digital)
4. Do NOT use if patient cannot cooperate

**Rectal (Most Accurate for Infants):**
1. Lubricate thermometer
2. Insert 1-2 cm into rectum
3. Hold in place for 2-3 minutes
4. Most accurate but invasive

### Temperature Classification

**Normal:** 97-99°F (36-37.2°C)
**Low-grade fever:** 99-100.4°F (37.2-38°C)
**Moderate fever:** 100.4-103°F (38-39.5°C)
**High fever:** 103-106°F (39.5-41°C)
**Hyperpyrexia:** >106°F (>41°C) - EMERGENCY

**Hypothermia:** <95°F (<35°C) - EMERGENCY

### Interpretation

**Fever Causes:**
- Infection (most common)
- Inflammation
- Heat stroke
- Medications
- Malignancy

**Hypothermia Causes:**
- Cold exposure
- Shock
- Severe malnutrition
- Sepsis in newborns

## 5. OXYGEN SATURATION (SpO2)

### Normal Ranges

| Population | Normal SpO2 | Low (Hypoxemia) |
|------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Healthy adults and children | 95-100% | <95% |
| Chronic lung disease | 88-92% | <88% |

### How to Measure (If Pulse Oximeter Available)

1. Clean finger (remove nail polish)
2. Attach probe to fingertip
3. Wait for reading to stabilize
4. Note percentage

### Interpretation

**Normal:** 95-100%
**Mild hypoxemia:** 90-94%
**Moderate hypoxemia:** 85-89%
**Severe hypoxemia:** <85% - EMERGENCY

**Causes of Low SpO2:**
- Pneumonia
- Asthma
- Heart failure
- Anemia
- High altitude

## 6. LEVEL OF CONSCIOUSNESS

### AVPU Scale (Quick Assessment)

**A - Alert:**
- Fully awake
- Responds appropriately
- Oriented to person, place, time

**V - Voice:**
- Responds to voice
- May be confused
- Opens eyes when called

**P - Pain:**
- Responds only to painful stimulus
- Does not respond to voice

**U - Unresponsive:**
- No response to any stimulus
- Unconscious

**Any level below "Alert" = EMERGENCY**

### Glasgow Coma Scale (More Detailed)

**Eye Opening:**
- Spontaneous: 4
- To voice: 3
- To pain: 2
- None: 1

**Verbal Response:**
- Oriented: 5
- Confused: 4
- Inappropriate words: 3
- Incomprehensible sounds: 2
- None: 1

**Motor Response:**
- Obeys commands: 6
- Localizes pain: 5
- Withdraws from pain: 4
- Abnormal flexion: 3
- Abnormal extension: 2
- None: 1

**Total Score:** 3-15
- 13-15: Mild impairment
- 9-12: Moderate impairment
- 3-8: Severe impairment (coma)

## 7. CAPILLARY REFILL TIME (CRT)

### How to Measure

1. Press on fingernail or palm for 5 seconds
2. Release pressure
3. Count seconds until color returns

### Interpretation

**Normal:** <2 seconds
**Delayed:** >2 seconds

**Causes of Delayed CRT:**
- Shock
- Dehydration
- Poor circulation
- Cold environment

---

## 8. SKIN TURGOR (Dehydration Assessment)

### How to Check

1. Pinch skin on back of hand or abdomen
2. Release
3. Observe how quickly skin returns to normal

### Interpretation

**Normal:** Returns immediately (<2 seconds)
**Mild dehydration:** Returns in 2-3 seconds
**Moderate dehydration:** Returns in 3-5 seconds
**Severe dehydration:** Returns very slowly (>5 seconds) or stays tented

## SIGNS OF SHOCK (Critical Emergency)

**Recognize shock by combination of:**
- Rapid, weak pulse (>100/min)
- Low blood pressure (<90 systolic)
- Rapid breathing (>20/min)
- Cold, clammy, pale skin
- Delayed capillary refill (>2 seconds)
- Confusion or decreased consciousness
- Decreased urine output
- Weak or thready pulse

**ALL signs of shock = CRITICAL EMERGENCY**

## WHEN TO REFER (Based on Vital Signs)

**IMMEDIATE REFERRAL (EMERGENCY):**
- Respiratory rate >30 (adult) or >50 (infant)
- Heart rate >120 (adult) or >160 (infant)
- Blood pressure <90/60 or >180/120
- Temperature >106°F or <95°F
- SpO2 <90%
- Unresponsive or responds only to pain
- Signs of shock

**URGENT REFERRAL:**
- Abnormal vital signs not in emergency range
- Persistent tachycardia or tachypnea
- Fever >103°F
- Blood pressure 140-180/90-120
- SpO2 90-94%
