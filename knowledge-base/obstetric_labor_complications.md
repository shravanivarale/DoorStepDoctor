# Obstetric Labor Complications Protocol

## Overview

Labor complications are a major cause of maternal and neonatal mortality in rural India. Early recognition and timely referral can save lives. ASHA workers play a critical role in identifying danger signs during labor.

## Normal Labor Duration

**First-time mothers (Primigravida):**
- First stage: 12-18 hours
- Second stage (pushing): 1-2 hours

**Mothers who have given birth before (Multigravida):**
- First stage: 6-12 hours
- Second stage: 30 minutes to 1 hour

**Any labor >24 hours = EMERGENCY**

---

## 1. PROLONGED LABOR

### Definition

- Labor lasting >12 hours in primigravida
- Labor lasting >8 hours in multigravida
- No progress in cervical dilation for 4 hours
- Pushing for >2 hours without delivery

### Causes

- Cephalopelvic disproportion (baby's head too large for pelvis)
- Abnormal fetal position
- Weak contractions
- Maternal exhaustion
- Dehydration

### Danger Signs

- Labor >12 hours
- Exhausted mother
- Weak or irregular contractions
- No progress despite strong contractions
- Fetal distress (see below)
- Maternal fever
- Foul-smelling vaginal discharge

### Emergency Criteria

**Classify as EMERGENCY if:**
- Labor >12 hours (primigravida) or >8 hours (multigravida)
- No progress for 4 hours
- Signs of fetal distress
- Maternal exhaustion
- Fever during labor

### Immediate Actions

1. **Assess progress:**
   - Time since labor started
   - Frequency and strength of contractions
   - Any progress in delivery

2. **Check fetal heart rate:**
   - Normal: 120-160 beats/minute
   - <120 or >160 = fetal distress

3. **Hydration:**
   - Give ORS or water by mouth
   - Keep mother hydrated

4. **Position:**
   - Encourage walking (if early labor)
   - Try different positions (squatting, hands-knees)

5. **Transport to PHC immediately** if:
   - >12 hours in labor
   - No progress
   - Any danger signs

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT push on abdomen
- Do NOT give any medications to speed labor
- Do NOT attempt delivery if obstructed
- Do NOT delay referral

---

## 2. OBSTRUCTED LABOR

### Definition

Labor where baby cannot pass through birth canal despite strong contractions. This is a LIFE-THREATENING EMERGENCY.

### Causes

- Cephalopelvic disproportion
- Abnormal fetal position (transverse lie)
- Large baby (macrosomia)
- Pelvic abnormalities

### Danger Signs (Bandl's Ring)

- Severe, continuous abdominal pain
- Distended lower abdomen
- Retraction ring visible on abdomen
- Bladder distension
- Blood in urine
- Cessation of contractions
- Maternal exhaustion and shock
- Fetal distress or death

### Emergency Criteria

**ALL cases of obstructed labor = CRITICAL EMERGENCY**

### Immediate Actions

1. **Recognize early:**
   - Prolonged labor with no progress
   - Severe pain between contractions
   - Visible retraction ring

2. **Do NOT attempt delivery**

3. **Position:** Left lateral position (on left side)

4. **IV fluids** if available and trained

5. **Empty bladder** with catheter if available and trained

6. **Transport IMMEDIATELY** to facility with cesarean section capability

7. **Call ahead** to prepare for emergency C-section

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT push on abdomen
- Do NOT give oxytocin or any labor-inducing drugs
- Do NOT attempt forceps or vacuum delivery
- Do NOT delay transport

---

## 3. BREECH PRESENTATION

### Definition

Baby positioned feet or buttocks first instead of head first. Occurs in 3-4% of pregnancies.

### Types

- **Frank breech:** Buttocks first, legs extended
- **Complete breech:** Buttocks first, legs folded
- **Footling breech:** One or both feet first

### Detection

- Palpation: Hard, round head felt in upper abdomen
- Soft, irregular buttocks in pelvis
- Fetal heart sounds heard above umbilicus

### Emergency Criteria

**ALL breech presentations in labor = EMERGENCY**

### Immediate Actions

1. **Identify breech early** (ideally before labor)

2. **Do NOT attempt delivery** at home or PHC

3. **Position:** Hands-knees position may help baby turn (if early labor)

4. **Transport immediately** to facility with cesarean capability

5. **Monitor fetal heart rate** during transport

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT attempt breech delivery unless trained obstetrician
- Do NOT pull on baby's legs or body
- Do NOT delay referral

---

## 4. CORD PROLAPSE

### Definition

Umbilical cord slips through cervix before baby. Cord gets compressed, cutting off baby's oxygen supply. This is a CRITICAL EMERGENCY.

### Risk Factors

- Breech presentation
- Premature rupture of membranes
- Polyhydramnios (excess amniotic fluid)
- Multiple pregnancy
- Premature labor

### Signs

- Cord visible or felt in vagina after membrane rupture
- Sudden fetal distress after membrane rupture

### Emergency Criteria

**Cord prolapse = CRITICAL EMERGENCY - Minutes matter!**

### Immediate Actions

1. **Position:** Knee-chest position or Trendelenburg (head down, hips elevated)
   - This relieves pressure on cord

2. **If cord visible:**
   - Cover with warm, moist cloth
   - Do NOT push cord back
   - Do NOT handle cord excessively

3. **If trained:** Insert two fingers in vagina and gently push baby's head up to relieve pressure on cord

4. **Give oxygen** if available

5. **Transport IMMEDIATELY** - maintain position during transport

6. **Call ahead** for emergency cesarean section

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT push cord back into uterus
- Do NOT allow mother to stand or sit upright
- Do NOT delay transport even for seconds

---

## 5. SHOULDER DYSTOCIA

### Definition

Baby's head delivers but shoulders get stuck behind mother's pubic bone. This is an EMERGENCY.

### Risk Factors

- Large baby (>4 kg)
- Maternal diabetes
- Prolonged second stage
- Previous shoulder dystocia

### Signs

- Head delivers but retracts back ("turtle sign")
- Body does not deliver with next contraction
- Difficulty delivering shoulders

### Emergency Criteria

**Shoulder dystocia = EMERGENCY**

### Immediate Actions (McRoberts Maneuver)

1. **Position:** Bring mother's knees to chest (hyperflexion of hips)

2. **Apply suprapubic pressure:**
   - Press down and to the side just above pubic bone
   - Do NOT push on abdomen

3. **If trained:** Internal maneuvers to rotate baby

4. **If delivery not achieved in 5 minutes:** Transport immediately

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT pull hard on baby's head (can cause nerve damage)
- Do NOT push on mother's abdomen (fundal pressure)
- Do NOT panic - stay calm and systematic

---

## 6. RETAINED PLACENTA

### Definition

Placenta not delivered within 30 minutes of baby's birth, or incomplete placenta delivery.

### Danger Signs

- Placenta not delivered after 30 minutes
- Heavy bleeding
- Pieces of placenta missing
- Cord breaks during controlled cord traction

### Emergency Criteria

**Classify as EMERGENCY if:**
- Placenta not delivered after 30 minutes
- Heavy bleeding
- Signs of shock

### Immediate Actions

1. **Wait 30 minutes** after delivery

2. **Controlled cord traction** (if trained):
   - Gentle, steady traction on cord
   - Counter-pressure on uterus
   - Do NOT pull hard

3. **If placenta delivers:**
   - Inspect for completeness
   - Check for missing pieces

4. **If not delivered or incomplete:**
   - Start IV fluids if available
   - Massage uterus
   - Transport to PHC for manual removal

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT pull hard on cord (can cause uterine inversion)
- Do NOT delay referral beyond 30 minutes

---

## 7. UTERINE RUPTURE

### Definition

Tear in uterine wall during labor. This is a LIFE-THREATENING EMERGENCY.

### Risk Factors

- Previous cesarean section
- Multiple pregnancies
- Obstructed labor
- Excessive use of oxytocin
- Trauma to abdomen

### Danger Signs

- Sudden, severe abdominal pain
- Cessation of contractions
- Vaginal bleeding
- Palpable fetal parts through abdomen
- Maternal shock
- Fetal distress or death

### Emergency Criteria

**Uterine rupture = CRITICAL EMERGENCY**

### Immediate Actions

1. **Recognize signs early**

2. **Start IV fluids** if available

3. **Position:** Flat, legs elevated

4. **Treat for shock**

5. **Transport IMMEDIATELY** for emergency surgery

6. **Call ahead** to prepare for emergency laparotomy

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT give oxytocin
- Do NOT delay transport

---

## 8. AMNIOTIC FLUID EMBOLISM

### Definition

Rare but catastrophic complication where amniotic fluid enters mother's bloodstream.

### Signs

- Sudden difficulty breathing
- Rapid heart rate
- Low blood pressure
- Seizures
- Cardiac arrest
- Heavy bleeding

### Emergency Criteria

**Amniotic fluid embolism = CRITICAL EMERGENCY**

### Immediate Actions

1. **Call for emergency help immediately**

2. **Position:** Left lateral

3. **Give oxygen** if available

4. **Be prepared for CPR**

5. **Transport immediately**

---

## FETAL DISTRESS - Common Complication

### Signs

**Fetal heart rate abnormalities:**
- Normal: 120-160 beats/minute
- Bradycardia: <120 beats/minute
- Tachycardia: >160 beats/minute

**Other signs:**
- Meconium-stained amniotic fluid (greenish)
- Decreased fetal movements
- Abnormal fetal heart rate patterns

### Actions

1. **Position mother on left side** (improves blood flow to baby)

2. **Give oxygen** if available

3. **Hydrate** with ORS or water

4. **Transport immediately** if fetal distress persists

## General Labor Danger Signs

**Refer IMMEDIATELY if any of these present:**

**Maternal:**
- Severe bleeding
- Severe headache or blurred vision
- Convulsions
- High fever (>100.4°F)
- Severe abdominal pain
- Difficulty breathing
- Loss of consciousness

**Fetal:**
- Abnormal fetal heart rate
- Meconium-stained fluid
- Decreased fetal movements
- Cord prolapse

**Labor:**
- Prolonged labor (>12 hours)
- Obstructed labor
- Abnormal presentation
- Ruptured membranes >24 hours

---

## Prevention and Preparation

**Antenatal Care:**
- Regular checkups during pregnancy
- Identify high-risk pregnancies
- Plan delivery location
- Arrange transport in advance

**Birth Preparedness:**
- Identify nearest facility with cesarean capability
- Arrange transport and funds
- Identify blood donors
- Prepare for emergencies

**Skilled Birth Attendance:**
- All deliveries should have skilled attendant
- Home deliveries only if low-risk and prepared
- Know when to refer

---

## Transport During Labor

**Preparation:**
- Call ahead to receiving facility
- Bring antenatal card and records
- Accompany with family member
- Maintain position as needed (e.g., knee-chest for cord prolapse)

**During Transport:**
- Monitor contractions
- Check fetal heart rate
- Watch for bleeding
- Keep mother hydrated
- Reassure and support

---

**DISCLAIMER:** This is a general triage guideline for ASHA workers. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or treatment. Always refer critical cases to the nearest PHC immediately.
