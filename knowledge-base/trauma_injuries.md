# Trauma and Injuries Protocol

## Overview

Trauma is a leading cause of death and disability in rural India, especially from road traffic accidents, falls, agricultural injuries, and violence. Quick assessment and appropriate first aid can save lives.

## Types of Trauma

### 1. Head Injuries
### 2. Chest Injuries
### 3. Abdominal Injuries
### 4. Fractures and Dislocations
### 5. Bleeding and Wounds
### 6. Spinal Injuries

---

## 1. HEAD INJURIES

### Assessment

**Mechanism of injury:**
- Fall from height
- Road traffic accident
- Blow to head
- Assault

**Level of consciousness (AVPU Scale):**
- **A**lert - fully awake and responsive
- **V**oice - responds to voice
- **P**ain - responds only to pain
- **U**nresponsive - no response

**Danger Signs:**
- Loss of consciousness (even if brief)
- Confusion or disorientation
- Severe headache
- Vomiting (especially repeated)
- Seizures
- Clear or bloody fluid from nose or ears
- Unequal pupils
- Weakness or numbness in limbs
- Slurred speech
- Drowsiness or difficulty waking

### Emergency Criteria

**Classify as EMERGENCY if:**
- Any loss of consciousness
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Vomiting after head injury
- Seizures
- Fluid leaking from nose or ears
- Unequal pupils
- Weakness in arms or legs
- Severe headache
- Infant or child with head injury

### Immediate Actions

1. **Check ABC:**
   - Airway - is it clear?
   - Breathing - is patient breathing?
   - Circulation - is there a pulse?

2. **Immobilize head and neck** (suspect spinal injury until proven otherwise)

3. **Control bleeding:**
   - Apply gentle pressure with clean cloth
   - Do NOT press hard on depressed skull fracture

4. **Position:**
   - If conscious: semi-reclined position
   - If unconscious but breathing: recovery position (on side)
   - If vomiting: turn to side to prevent choking

5. **Monitor consciousness** every 5 minutes

6. **Transport immediately** to PHC

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT give anything by mouth
- Do NOT move patient unnecessarily
- Do NOT remove objects embedded in skull
- Do NOT give pain medication (can mask symptoms)

## 2. CHEST INJURIES

### Types

**Blunt chest trauma:**
- Rib fractures
- Lung contusion
- Pneumothorax (collapsed lung)

**Penetrating chest trauma:**
- Stab wounds
- Impalement

### Danger Signs

- Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
- Rapid, shallow breathing
- Chest pain (especially with breathing)
- Coughing up blood
- Unequal chest movement
- Sucking chest wound (air entering through wound)
- Distended neck veins
- Cyanosis (blue lips/fingernails)
- Signs of shock

### Emergency Criteria

**ALL chest injuries = EMERGENCY**

### Immediate Actions

1. **Position:** Sit patient upright or semi-reclined (easier to breathe)

2. **For sucking chest wound:**
   - Cover immediately with plastic or non-porous material
   - Tape on 3 sides only (creates one-way valve)
   - Allows air out but not in

3. **For impaled object:**
   - Do NOT remove
   - Stabilize object with padding around it
   - Bandage in place

4. **Monitor breathing** continuously

5. **Give oxygen** if available

6. **Transport immediately** - sitting position if conscious

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT remove impaled objects
- Do NOT seal chest wound completely (can cause tension pneumothorax)
- Do NOT lay patient flat if breathing difficulty

## 3. ABDOMINAL INJURIES

### Types

**Blunt abdominal trauma:**
- Internal bleeding
- Organ damage (liver, spleen, kidney)

**Penetrating abdominal trauma:**
- Stab wounds
- Impalement

### Danger Signs

- Severe abdominal pain
- Rigid, board-like abdomen
- Distended abdomen
- Bruising on abdomen
- Vomiting blood
- Blood in urine or stool
- Signs of shock
- Protruding organs (evisceration)

### Emergency Criteria

**ALL significant abdominal injuries = EMERGENCY**

### Immediate Actions

1. **Position:** Lie flat with knees bent (reduces abdominal tension)

2. **For protruding organs:**
   - Do NOT push back inside
   - Cover with clean, moist cloth
   - Do NOT touch organs

3. **For impaled object:**
   - Do NOT remove
   - Stabilize in place

4. **Nothing by mouth** (no food or water)

5. **Monitor for shock**

6. **Transport immediately**

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT give anything to eat or drink
- Do NOT push protruding organs back
- Do NOT remove impaled objects
- Do NOT apply pressure to protruding organs

## 4. FRACTURES AND DISLOCATIONS

### Signs of Fracture

- Pain at injury site
- Swelling and bruising
- Deformity or abnormal position
- Unable to move or bear weight
- Grinding sensation (crepitus)
- Bone visible through skin (open fracture)

### Types

**Closed fracture:** Bone broken but skin intact
**Open fracture:** Bone visible through skin - HIGH RISK of infection

### Emergency Criteria

**Classify as EMERGENCY if:**
- Open fracture (bone visible)
- Fracture of skull, spine, pelvis, or femur (thigh bone)
- Multiple fractures
- Severe deformity
- Loss of pulse or sensation below fracture
- Severe bleeding

**Urgent (refer to PHC):**
- All other fractures

### Immediate Actions

1. **Immobilize the fracture:**
   - Splint in position found (don't try to straighten)
   - Include joints above and below fracture
   - Use rigid materials (wood, rolled newspaper, etc.)
   - Pad splint with cloth
   - Tie securely but not too tight

2. **For open fracture:**
   - Cover wound with clean cloth
   - Do NOT push bone back in
   - Immobilize as above
   - Classify as EMERGENCY

3. **Check circulation:**
   - Feel pulse below fracture
   - Check skin color and temperature
   - Ask about sensation

4. **Elevate if possible** (reduces swelling)

5. **Apply ice** (wrapped in cloth, not directly on skin)

6. **Transport to PHC**

### Specific Fractures

**Femur (thigh bone):**
- Can cause severe internal bleeding
- Classify as EMERGENCY
- Immobilize entire leg
- Monitor for shock

**Pelvis:**
- Can cause massive internal bleeding
- Classify as EMERGENCY
- Do NOT move patient
- Call for ambulance

**Spine:**
- See spinal injuries section below
- Do NOT move patient

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT try to straighten deformed limb
- Do NOT push bone back through skin
- Do NOT tie splint too tight (cuts off circulation)
- Do NOT give anything by mouth if surgery may be needed

## 5. BLEEDING AND WOUNDS

### Types of Bleeding

**Arterial:** Bright red, spurting blood - MOST DANGEROUS
**Venous:** Dark red, steady flow
**Capillary:** Oozing, minor bleeding

### Bleeding Control (Step-by-Step)

**Step 1: Direct Pressure**
- Apply firm, direct pressure with clean cloth
- Hold for 10-15 minutes without lifting
- If blood soaks through, add more cloth on top (don't remove first layer)
- Elevate injured part above heart level if possible

**Step 2: Pressure Points (if Step 1 fails)**
- Apply pressure to artery supplying the area
- Arm: brachial artery (inside of upper arm)
- Leg: femoral artery (groin)

**Step 3: Tourniquet (ONLY for life-threatening limb bleeding)**
- Use ONLY if direct pressure fails and bleeding is life-threatening
- Apply 2-3 inches above wound (not on joint)
- Tighten until bleeding stops
- Note time applied
- Do NOT loosen once applied
- Transport immediately

### Emergency Criteria

**Classify as EMERGENCY if:**
- Severe bleeding (soaking through multiple cloths)
- Arterial bleeding (spurting)
- Bleeding not controlled by direct pressure
- Signs of shock
- Bleeding from multiple sites
- Bleeding with other serious injuries

### Wound Care

**For minor wounds:**
1. Clean with clean water
2. Apply pressure to stop bleeding
3. Cover with clean dressing
4. Refer to PHC for tetanus shot if needed

**For major wounds:**
1. Control bleeding first
2. Do NOT clean (risk of increasing bleeding)
3. Cover with clean cloth
4. Transport to PHC

**For impaled objects:**
- Do NOT remove
- Stabilize object in place
- Bandage around object
- Transport to PHC

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT use tourniquet unless absolutely necessary
- Do NOT remove impaled objects
- Do NOT probe wounds
- Do NOT apply herbs or traditional remedies

## 6. SPINAL INJURIES

### Suspect Spinal Injury if:

- Fall from height (>3 feet)
- High-speed motor vehicle accident
- Diving accident
- Blow to head or neck
- Unconscious after trauma
- Neck or back pain after injury
- Tingling or numbness in limbs
- Weakness or inability to move limbs

### Danger Signs

- Severe neck or back pain
- Inability to move arms or legs
- Loss of sensation
- Tingling or numbness
- Loss of bladder or bowel control
- Difficulty breathing
- Twisted or deformed neck or back

### Emergency Criteria

**ALL suspected spinal injuries = EMERGENCY**

### Immediate Actions

1. **Do NOT move patient** unless in immediate danger

2. **Immobilize head and neck:**
   - Hold head in neutral position (straight)
   - Have someone maintain this position continuously
   - Use rolled towels or clothing on both sides of head

3. **If must move:**
   - Need 4-5 people
   - Keep head, neck, and body in straight line
   - Move as one unit (log roll technique)

4. **Monitor breathing** - spinal injury can affect breathing

5. **Call for ambulance** - specialized transport needed

6. **Reassure patient** - tell them not to move

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT move patient unless absolutely necessary
- Do NOT bend or twist spine
- Do NOT allow patient to sit up
- Do NOT remove helmet (if motorcyclist) unless trained

## SHOCK - Common Complication of Trauma

### Signs of Shock

- Pale, cold, clammy skin
- Rapid, weak pulse (>100/min)
- Rapid, shallow breathing
- Confusion or anxiety
- Decreased urine output
- Thirst
- Weakness or dizziness

### Management

1. **Lay patient flat** (unless chest injury or difficulty breathing)
2. **Elevate legs** 12 inches (unless spinal injury suspected)
3. **Keep warm** with blankets
4. **Do NOT give anything by mouth**
5. **Monitor vital signs** every 5 minutes
6. **Transport immediately**

## General Trauma Assessment (Primary Survey)

**Use ABC approach:**

**A - Airway:**
- Is airway clear?
- Remove visible obstructions
- Maintain cervical spine protection

**B - Breathing:**
- Is patient breathing?
- Respiratory rate and effort
- Chest movement equal on both sides?

**C - Circulation:**
- Is there a pulse?
- Severe bleeding?
- Signs of shock?

**D - Disability:**
- Level of consciousness (AVPU)
- Pupil size and reaction

**E - Exposure:**
- Remove clothing to assess injuries
- Keep patient warm

---

## Transport Priority

**IMMEDIATE (RED):**
- Airway obstruction
- Severe bleeding
- Chest injuries
- Head injuries with altered consciousness
- Spinal injuries
- Shock
- Multiple injuries

**URGENT (YELLOW):**
- Fractures (closed)
- Moderate bleeding
- Burns

**NON-URGENT (GREEN):**
- Minor wounds
- Minor fractures (fingers, toes)

## Prevention Counseling

**Road Safety:**
- Wear helmets on motorcycles
- Use seat belts in vehicles
- Avoid drunk driving
- Follow speed limits

**Home Safety:**
- Secure ladders properly
- Keep floors dry and uncluttered
- Use proper footwear

**Agricultural Safety:**
- Use protective equipment
- Maintain machinery properly
- Avoid working alone in remote areas
