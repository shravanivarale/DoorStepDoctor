# Burns Management Guidelines

## Assessment

**Burn Severity Classification:**
- **First Degree:** Red, painful, no blisters (like sunburn) - affects outer skin layer only
- **Second Degree:** Blisters, very painful, red/white appearance - affects deeper skin layers
- **Third Degree:** White/charred/leathery, may not be painful (nerve damage) - full thickness burn

## Rule of Nines (Body Surface Area Estimation)

**Adults:**
- Head and neck: 9%
- Each arm: 9% (both arms = 18%)
- Front of chest: 18%
- Back: 18%
- Each leg: 18% (both legs = 36%)
- Genitals: 1%

**Children (adjust for larger head):**
- Head: 18%
- Each arm: 9%
- Front: 18%
- Back: 18%
- Each leg: 14%

**Quick Method:** Patient's palm (including fingers) = approximately 1% of body surface area

## Immediate Actions (First Aid)

1. **Remove from heat source** - Stop the burning process immediately
2. **Cool with running water** for 10-20 minutes (use clean water, room temperature)
3. **Remove jewelry and tight clothing** before swelling starts (do NOT remove if stuck to burn)
4. **Cover with clean, dry cloth** or sterile dressing (do NOT use cotton wool)
5. **Elevate burned area** if possible to reduce swelling

## Emergency Criteria (Refer to PHC IMMEDIATELY)

**Classify as EMERGENCY if:**
- Burns covering >10% body surface area (adults) or >5% (children)
- Burns on face, hands, feet, genitals, joints, or major joints
- Third-degree burns of any size
- Electrical burns (internal damage may be severe)
- Chemical burns (especially eyes, face, or large area)
- Inhalation injury signs:
  - Burnt nose hairs
  - Soot in mouth or nose
  - Difficulty breathing
  - Hoarse voice
- Burns in children <5 years or elderly >60 years
- Circumferential burns (completely around arm, leg, or chest)
- Signs of shock:
  - Pale, cold, clammy skin
  - Rapid, weak pulse
  - Confusion or drowsiness
  - Rapid breathing

## Fluid Management (For Transport >1 Hour)

**If burn >15% BSA and transport will take >1 hour:**
- Give ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution) by mouth if patient is conscious and can swallow
- **Adults:** 500ml per hour
- **Children:** 250ml per hour
- Continue until reaching PHC

**Signs of dehydration to watch:**
- Decreased urine output
- Dry mouth
- Increased thirst
- Dizziness

## Age-Specific Considerations

**Infants and Children:**
- ANY burn >5% BSA = EMERGENCY
- Higher risk of shock and dehydration
- Faster heat loss - keep warm during transport
- More prone to infection

**Elderly:**
- Thinner skin - deeper burns more likely
- Slower healing
- Higher risk of complications
- Lower threshold for hospital referral

**Pregnant Women:**
- Any significant burn = refer to PHC
- Risk to both mother and fetus
- Increased fluid requirements

## What NOT to Do (Common Mistakes)

**NEVER:**
- Apply ice directly (causes further tissue damage and hypothermia)
- Apply butter, oil, ghee, toothpaste, or any home remedies (increases infection risk)
- Break blisters intentionally (removes protective barrier)
- Use cotton wool directly on burn (fibers stick to wound)
- Remove clothing stuck to burn (will cause more damage)
- Give anything by mouth if patient is unconscious
- Delay transport for severe burns

## Transport Instructions

**Preparation:**
1. Cover burn with clean, dry cloth or sterile dressing
2. Do NOT apply any creams or ointments
3. Keep patient warm (but don't overheat)
4. Position comfortably - elevate burned limbs if possible
5. Monitor breathing and consciousness continuously

**During Transport:**
- Reassure patient and keep calm
- Give ORS if conscious and burn >15% BSA
- Watch for signs of shock
- Be prepared for vomiting
- Note time of burn and first aid given

## Special Types of Burns

**Electrical Burns:**
- May look minor on surface but have severe internal damage
- ALWAYS classify as EMERGENCY
- Check for entry and exit wounds
- Monitor heart rhythm if possible

**Chemical Burns:**
- Brush off dry chemicals first (don't use water initially)
- Then flush with large amounts of water for 20+ minutes
- Remove contaminated clothing carefully
- EMERGENCY referral for all chemical burns

**Scalds (Hot Liquids):**
- Common in children
- Usually second-degree burns
- Follow same cooling and assessment protocol

## Pain Management

**While waiting for transport:**
- Cooling with water provides pain relief
- Keep burn covered (exposure to air increases pain)
- Paracetamol can be given if available:
  - Adults: 500-1000mg
  - Children: 10-15mg/kg
- Reassurance and calm environment help reduce pain perception

## Infection Prevention

**Critical for all burns:**
- Use only clean/sterile materials for covering
- Do NOT touch burn with bare hands
- Avoid contamination from soil, saliva, or dirty water
- Cover burn completely before transport
- Tetanus prophylaxis will be needed at PHC

## Follow-Up Instructions (For Minor Burns Managed at Home)

**First-degree burns and small second-degree burns (<2% BSA) can be managed at home:**

1. Keep clean and dry
2. Change dressing daily
3. Watch for infection signs:
   - Increased redness spreading beyond burn
   - Increased pain after 2-3 days
   - Pus or foul smell
   - Fever
4. Return to PHC if any infection signs appear
5. Healing time: 1-2 weeks for first-degree, 2-3 weeks for second-degree

---
**DISCLAIMER:** This is a general triage guideline for ASHA workers. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or treatment. Always refer critical cases to the nearest PHC immediately.
